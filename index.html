Welcome

Thesis: Through her use of Symbolism, Characterization, Allusion and Dichotomy in Dispossessed, Désirée Zamorano argues that the expectation of assimilation into the "White" American culture systematically erases immigrant identity by discouraging expression of heritage, language, and customs.

About this project

This multimodal site combines literary analysis with visual storytelling through photos, artwork, and videography to explore themes of assimilation, erasure, and resistance in Zamorano’s work. Navigate the tabs above to experience three selected quotes alongside curated visuals and historical context.

Quote 1

“I told you something, boy. Get off of that goddamned swing. You Mexis shouldn't be out after sunset. Here in Rosewood, we don't want you here, we don't want you in the daylight either.” (Zamorano 71)

Historical Context

This quote reflects the generational divide within many Mexican-American communities and the outright racial exclusion that existed in certain neighborhoods. It echoes practices like “sundown towns” and the Mexican repatriation pressures of the 1930s, when Mexican and Mexican-American people were pushed away from White neighborhoods and public spaces. Rosewood functions as a literary stand-in for those exclusionary places.

Literary Devices

Characterization: The man’s voice represents both personal prejudice and the authority of discriminatory social norms.

Dichotomy: “Daylight vs. night” signals visible citizenship vs. forced invisibility.

Quote 2

“You tell them Lizette's French. You tell them Galvan is Spanish, from Galicia. Anything but Mexican!” (Zamorano 223)

Historical Context

This line captures the pressure on Mexican-American families to “pass” as European in order to gain social acceptance and avoid discrimination. In Los Angeles history, practices like racial passing and the erasure of Mexican neighborhoods (for example, the later removal of Chavez Ravine) show how identity could be a barrier to housing, work, and social mobility.

Literary Devices

Allusion: The line gestures toward broader erasures of Hispanic communities (e.g., Chavez Ravine).

Dichotomy: The contrast between “Mexican” and “Spanish/French” underscores racialized hierarchies of acceptability.

Quote 3

“I don't want you to waste your time in those math classes you'd have to take. They're way beyond your abilitie.” (Zamorano 87)

Historical Context

This line represents educational gatekeeping that has historically limited opportunities for Latino students. Contemporary data (e.g., AP participation reports) show Hispanic students are still underrepresented in advanced coursework, a pattern that literature like Zamorano's exposes by dramatizing teacher discouragement and lowered expectations.

Literary Devices

Characterization: The teacher's discouraging tone reveals institutional bias rather than a true measure of ability.

Symbolism: The “math classes” symbolize routes to social mobility and academic validation.
